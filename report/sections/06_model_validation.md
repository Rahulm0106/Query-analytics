# Section 6 - Model Validation

Generalization is assessed on held-out 20% test sets (40,000 Range rows; 2,000 Radius rows; seed = 42). All metrics are back-transformed to the original Count scale via `expm1()`. Train and test metrics for every model are exported in `results/final_benchmark.csv`.

All four model families generalize without obvious overfitting. The train to test RMSE gap is largest for Random Forest (Range: 3,438 → 7,801; RF memorizes training data) and smallest for XGBoost (5,775 → 6,575). Linear regression is stable across splits but offers a weak baseline, especially on Radius (test R² = 0.315), confirming that the spatial-query to count relationship is strongly non-linear. XGBoost is the test RMSE winner on both datasets; see Table 1 in §7.

**Diagnostics (figures 8–10).** *Residuals (fig08)*: XGBoost log-scale residuals are centered near zero with mild fan-out at the highest predicted log_Count (limited upper-tail heteroscedasticity, no systematic mean bias); predicted vs. actual panels lie tightly along y = x for both datasets. Bias by area decile (fig09, bias_by_decile.csv): test RMSE rises monotonically with log_area, from 1,411 in decile 1 to 10,859 in decile 10 (7.7× spread). Largest-area queries absorb most absolute error, consistent with the EDA finding that area dominates Count (ρ = 0.95). MAPE inversely collapses with area (65.5% → 1.9%), reflecting the difficulty of relative predicCounts. Spatial bias (fig10, bias_by_cluster.csv): K-Means on (X_norm, Y_norm, log_area_norm) selects K\* = 3 by silhouette (0.44 on a 5,000-row sample). Per-cluster XGBoost RMSE varies by \~43% across the three Chicago zones (5,370 → 7,653), confirming non-trivial spatial residual structure. The highest-error cluster also carries the highest boundary_flag prevalence (0.86%), providing partial evidence that boundary-crossing queries are a residual-risk segment.

**Risks and caveats:** XGBoost hit the `nrounds` cap during grid search (500 rounds for Range, 300 for Radius); the CV RMSE was still decreasing, so reported test RMSE is an upper bound. A longer training schedule may yield further improvement. Tree-based and KNN models cannot extrapolate beyond the training feature range, so out-of-distribution queries (extreme areas, off-grid coordinates, or query footprints far outside the Chicago bounding box) carry higher prediction risk. The 99.9% boundary-crossing rate in the Radius sub-dataset means the boundary_flag feature is not informative for Radius and was excluded. Production deployments on workloads with mixed boundary characteristics would warrant a Radius-specific retrain.
